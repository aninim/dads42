## ROUND 1 — THE ORIGIN STORY

### Q1: You didn't have a strong male role model. What was that absence like — concretely?

**Oren:**
Every part of my life was different. When I was very young, I had everything I think — we traveled a lot. My father took pictures of us. We traveled, camped, it was beautiful — the beach, we had a dog. Everything was quiet.

As we grew up, the city got more condensed. My two older brothers were busy — they were teenagers. My father worked, he left early when serving in the army. He'd come home around 6pm but we wouldn't do much together in the evening. I missed the father figure when I was a teenager, definitely when I was an adult.

My mom was the leading voice in the house. She was quite vocal. We were three boys, and later a sister. My parents pulled through everything to get money and provide what they thought we needed.

In my older, elderly life, I was more confused. I didn't know. I'm still looking exactly to know who I am. Kind of an "aha" moment, I guess. I missed it. I can look at my friends or other people and see exactly what they have. But I've been very careful, and vice versa. That's mostly it.

---

### Q2: When your first son was born, what was the moment you realized "I have no manual for this"?

**Oren:**
When I saw him behaving like me — sometimes a bit lost. Sometimes I was pushing him, trying to help too much. Not letting him be his own way.

When he was born, I felt the opposite of lost — I felt I knew everything. It felt so natural, like it's part of the universe, and it's in me without even reading one book. I felt I know everything, and I acted. I did everything for me. Guiding him more verbally, more educationally, more structured — giving him knowledge and expectations.

But then I saw myself in him. His confusion, his lostness. And I realized: *I'm replicating what I absorbed.*

I said I need to stop.

**Deeper layer (follow-up):**
It's both. Obviously, when you're frustrated about what to answer or how to guide them — and on the other hand, I understood: I need to learn. I need to grow. Because they're going to replicate me. They're going to absorb everything I am without me even opening my mouth.

So I had to look at my ways and what I'm missing. Because I want my kids to be whole people. Hopefully they can take care of themselves and know what's good for themselves.

That's why I sat down and opened this community — first for me, to grow and learn as I go. And whoever wants to join, it's better.

---

### Q3: What was the loneliness like?

**Oren:**
At first, it was the lonely nights when we shared the day — I was doing date nights or early nights with the babies. So quite lonely watching TV or whatever.

And having three kids under two — which is a blast — but it doesn't leave much time or energy for socializing or taking care of yourself. With very little sleep for a long time, it affects my work and my stress.

You feel alone because everyone expects you to be having normal, but no one really... People ask "how are you feeling?" but it's surface level. It's the surface on the surface.

## ROUND 2 — THE CRACK (Anna Machin & Marcus Aurelius)

### Q4: What exactly did Anna Machin's research unlock for you?

**Oren:**
I first encountered her on a podcast. It was amazing to see an academic persona, qualified, could do whatever she wants, and she dedicated her life to study us — to study me. That's big reassurance. It's not like a committee or a master degree research where you just pick one and move on. She actually dedicated her life.

When I read her book many, many times, it all came together. It's a very interesting story — an important part of the universe, at least for humans. I was really, really blown by it. Everything in the book was reassurance, something to take on and magnify. And I wanted to tell people around me, men around me, fathers to be.

**Additional layer:**
From young age, I looked to the stars. I knew there's something bigger, and we're affected by anything around us, whether we know it or not. Little things to the moon, to the sun — it was always with me.

When I traveled after the army, I read Hitchhiker's Guide, and that blew me away. It's another part of the universe — the same universe, only a different view.

Things click. That's what I want to convey. I'm looking at the universe level. I don't care about the nitty gritty. I'm looking at it on a holistic approach.

---

### Q5: Marcus Aurelius lost 5 kids and still showed up. Why Aurelius?

**Oren:**
Because of the situation, the context, the era, the magnitude of things — everything was to the max. There was no one else on this planet who went through this kind of life, and definitely not lived it the way he did — at least in his mind, in his Meditations.

This is something very unique and definitely something to look up to.

It's the implementation of the story. It's not just the theory. It's the implementation on the highest level. There's no second to that — the second is way, way below.

---

### Q6: How did Anna Machin and Marcus Aurelius connect for you?

**Oren:**
The universe and biological evolution is what made him. He's the example. He's the best example of that.

In short: he's the proof that evolution built us for this. Not just theoretically — he's the living example of a man whose brain was rewired for fatherhood, and he used it at the absolute highest stakes.

## ROUND 3 — WHY NOW, WHY YOU

### Q7: Why didn't you just keep this private?

**Oren:**
I think it's because of the dispute. We need each other. Everyone has some experience, some knowledge, something to share.

Tackling this universe alone with no manual — I could make it, but it would take me years to get there. That's one thing.

Another thing: it's difficult to have everything together as a father. So many things to take care of — in the house, at work, with kids — and still be strong. You need a reminder. You need a friend. You need a shoulder. Even if it's occasional — a post or coming up to a call — it can give a lot.

I'm sure everyone has something to share from their own experience. One can learn just from his idea.

The main goal: I don't have all the answers, but I think together, we have a very good chance to at least increase each person's success rate and have a happy life, happy family. Kids to have his friends and grow with the years.

---

### Q8: Who are you building this for — and who are you pointedly NOT building this for?

**Oren:**
It's for men like me. I wouldn't deny any moms to come along, obviously. But it's first for men. Maybe only men would feel comfortable because there is a difference. Maybe we also need to talk about the women in the room, the mother, the partner. So maybe it's really more comfortable for it to be men only.

**Deeper:**
Men like me — fathers to be or fathers, whatever age or gender or whatever challenge they might have. But the silver lining: they *know* they have a deficit. They don't shy away from it. They look at their deficits because they want to grow.

The growing part is a bit more difficult than chatting with an AI. You really need human support, human example, sharing.

I'm sure many men are in my situation — having little babies, not having time to leave the house even. They could value such a thing. It's more than just a thread. It's something we look for knowledge to learn actively and try and put ourselves in different habits.

Not everyone is ready for that or willing to do that.

On the other hand, it's not for people or men who want to just be listeners on the sidelines. I mean, it's okay, but I believe in being active in order to get or earn something. If you want something, there's something you need to do — almost always.

I don't want people just coming and doing nothing or thinking that just by reading or listening, they will grow. I don't think it will be for them. They are welcome, but I don't think they will gain what they think we can.

**Line in the sand:**
People who don't believe in themselves or don't see the universe connection to all of it. That everything affects everything else.
