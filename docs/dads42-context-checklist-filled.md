## SECTION 01 — YOUR PERSONAL STORY

### Personal Background

**Public name:** Oren Elimelech. Full name, no handle.

**Where grew up:** Ashdod, Israel. First 6 years in a basic apartment — immigrant family, not much money but all basics covered. Age 6: moved to a cottage, first row on the sea. Beautiful, calming. Parents came to Israel as young teenagers. City grew fast around them. Stable but with the texture of immigrant-family challenges. The beach was always the anchor.

**Age:** 47. Born July 13, 1978.

**Kids:** Three boys.
- Oldest: ~5.5 years old (born Nov 2020)
- Twins (non-identical): ~3.75 years old (born July 20, 2022)
- When twins arrived, oldest was ~20 months → three kids under two.

**Family structure:** Married.

**Relationship with own father:** Beautiful early years — travel, camping, beach, a dog. Everything was calm. As Oren grew into teenage years, father worked long hours, served in the army, came home around 6pm but they wouldn't do much together. Mom became the leading voice in the house. Three boys, then a sister. Parents pushed through everything to provide. Oren missed the father figure as a teenager, and even more as an adult. Still searching to know who he is.

**Key formative moment:** Beautiful early childhood → city condensing, brothers busy as teenagers, father receding → absence of a male role model in adolescence → confusion about identity that carried into adulthood and fatherhood.

**When/how became a father:** At first, felt completely natural — knew everything, like it was in him without reading one book. Acted with confidence. Guided his oldest more verbally, educationally, structured. Then saw himself in his son — his confusion, his lostness. Realized: *I'm replicating what I absorbed.* Said "I need to stop."

### The Turning Point

**Moment he realized he was a self-guided dad:** Seeing himself in his oldest son. The replication fear. Building without a blueprint.

**Biggest failure/regret:** Losing his temper — stress-triggered, sometimes physical (flap on the wrist). Always apologized. The trend is going down — he's proud of that. But with the eldest specifically, he's aware he's affecting his son's self-confidence. "I'm kind of in the way."

**What keeps him up at night:**
1. Education system won't challenge his kids — they're already bored in kindergarten
2. Not enough time to fill the gaps: teach them about life, the universe, how to walk the earth, how to communicate themselves
3. Passing down bad habits — especially bad communication habits

**What 'self-guided' means to him:** Building without a blueprint. No one showed him the way — he's figuring it out in real time, with his kids watching.

**What he knows now that he wishes he'd known:** "To be a decent father — not great, not an example, just decent — you have to grow first. And you never stop. Daily, hourly. Because each kid needs a completely different version of you, and you can't split yourself."

### Professional Context

**Career:** Data & Validation Team Leader at Gentex Israel (computer vision). Background in Product Management, System Engineering, Hardware R&D. Two patents (PetPace). He thinks like an engineer and a PM — systems thinker, not a coder.

**Formal education relevant to fatherhood:** No serious formal education. Attended a few parent guidance sessions. One structured: 12 sessions with a guide from the Adler Institute in Israel. Adlerian psychology framework — encouragement, autonomy, natural consequences, democratic parenting. This quietly underpins his approach even if he doesn't always name it.

**How long working on Dads'42:** The vision was brewing when the twins arrived (~3.5 years ago). About 1 year ago it became active: opened the Skool community, took Dan Koe's writing bootcamp, tried LinkedIn posts. Felt cheesy and misaligned with his voice — lost momentum. Tried filming himself — not comfortable on camera. Now with Claude AI as companion/partner, he has the confidence and scaffolding to push forward.

**Coached other dads:** Informally yes — friends, colleagues, a grandfather who found value in a long conversation. No formal coaching role yet.

## SECTION 02 — YOUR FATHERHOOD EXPERIENCE

### Specific Stories & Moments

**Lost his temper:** Many times. Pattern is stress-triggered — when he's in his own tantrum. Sometimes went physical (flap on wrist). Always apologized. Trend going down. Most impactful on his eldest, who needs more self-confidence. "I'm kind of in the way."

**Got it exactly right:** Rafael (the older of the twins) struggles with feeling small against his brothers — copies everything they want. At a snack bar, on the way out, Rafael wanted something different from what he'd gotten. Oren told him: "We can do that, but we'll need to wait the whole line again." Rafael said yes. Oren checked his watch and said: "I think we'll miss the next activity if we wait." Rafael thought for a few seconds, then let it go. They moved on. No tantrum, no power struggle. Oren gave him the information and let him make the call. Clean autonomy + natural consequences. Rafael left that moment feeling respected, not denied.

**Biggest fear (3am version):** Education system won't challenge them (already bored in kindergarten). Not enough time to fill the gaps — life, universe, how to communicate. Passing down bad communication habits.

**Thing nobody warned him about:** "To be a decent father — not great, not an example, just decent — you have to grow first. And you have to gain the habit of growing always — daily, hourly — and adjust to give each kid the unique approach they individually need. My kids are very close in age. I cannot split myself to give each one what they need. That's my big struggle right now."

**How fatherhood changed his identity:** Saw himself in his son. Understood his own father — both his shortcomings AND the wonderful, unique things he passed on. Both sides. That's the redemption arc: replication fear → self-examination → appreciation of what was given alongside what was missing.

### Dad Superpowers (per Machin's research)

**What he's naturally good at:** Staying composed and following steps to diffuse a situation. When he does it — it works beautifully.

**Day-to-day with kids:** Three boys very close in age. The constant challenge is splitting himself to meet each one's individual needs. That's his active struggle.

**Parenting philosophy (one sentence):** "Embrace the challenge, cherish the moments."

**What fathers uniquely provide:** Risk-taking. Showing kids how to deal with problems. How to manage temper. How to feel comfortable in the uncomfortable. How to explore beyond the obvious. And modeling: respect for women, what family means, what brotherhood means, respect for parents.

## SECTION 03 — INTELLECTUAL INFLUENCES

### Anna Machin — The Science

**Insight that hit hardest:** "No matter how many mothers a baby has, it will never fulfill the father's contribution." Nature has no redundancy — it hates redundancy. Fathers exist because evolution requires them. Not backup mothers — something irreplaceable. As an engineer, this clicked immediately: no system builds in a component it doesn't need. This was the scientific permission he'd been looking for. Not just feeling — proof.

**How Machin changed him:** Reassurance that his role is built into the universe. Wanted to tell every father he knew. Also: she dedicated her *life* to studying fathers specifically — that weight matters. It's not a side project. Someone gave their career to proving you matter.

**Which Machin insight most dads haven't heard:** The irreplaceability point. Fathers aren't optional or supplementary. Evolution wouldn't build in redundancy. You are structurally necessary.

### Marcus Aurelius — The Philosophy

**Why Aurelius:** The magnitude. The context. No one else on earth went through what he did and still showed up — losing 5 kids, running an empire. It's not theory. It's implementation at the highest possible stakes. The second place is way, way below.

**Stoic question he asks himself:** "Have I been the person I want my kids to be?" — Am I as positive as I can be about life and people? Am I avoiding heating up over stupid things (road rage, small provocations)?

**How Machin and Aurelius connect:** Aurelius is the proof of concept. Machin shows the biology; Aurelius shows what a man built by that biology can do under maximum pressure.

### Other Influences

**Douglas Adams / Hitchhiker's Guide to the Galaxy:** Cemented his voice and worldview. The humor, the optimism, the idea that everything has an answer in the form of the universe — and everything works out in the end. "Don't panic." Liberating. Permission to be naughty while clinging to something eternal. "You might as well enjoy the ride."

**James Hunt (the F1 driver / the film Rush):** "Sex: Breakfast of Champions" — unapologetic, fully alive, owns what he loves. One leg in the childish and naughty, one leg in the eternal and universe-aligned. That duality is his personality.

**Adlerian psychology:** 12 structured sessions, Adler Institute Israel. Encouragement, autonomy, natural consequences, democratic family — runs quietly underneath the Dads'42 philosophy.

**Original Framework:**
- Developed his own top-level framework starting from: *what do I actually want my kids to BE?*
- Not careers (not doctor, lawyer, pilot)
- The one goal: the ability to communicate themselves to the universe, to people, to the world — to express their wants and needs clearly. That calibrates them as they go. If they talk rubbish, reality mirrors it back. If they do right, the universe rewards them.
- Built on Pillars (documented separately in project files — pull `docs/` for the full list)

## SECTION 04 — TARGET AUDIENCE

### Who He Is

**No structural gatekeeping:** Single, divorced, co-parenting, married, father-to-be, grandfather — all welcome. The community needs rooms for everyone. A grandfather who felt he'd missed something for years and only now has clarity to deal with it — that's also the Dads'42 member.

**Primary avatar (the face in his head when he writes):** Himself a year ago. Married, three young kids, exhausted, deep in the family, missing himself. Feeling the gap. Nobody asking how *he* is doing.

**The real filter — not demographics:** THE SPARK. Someone who knows something's off and has already taken at least one step toward fixing it. Not passive consumers. Not people who think reading or listening will change them automatically. People who've tried 1-2-3 things, maybe failed, and are coming with real problems — even if they can't frame the problem yet. The pillars give them the language to frame what they're carrying.

**His average evening:** Full spectrum. Post-workout juice guy — house clean, everything working, but something's still missing. All the way to chaos-house guy — kids screaming, wife upset, ten things to do, no idea where to start, can't handle himself. Both are in Dads'42. Posts have to work for both simultaneously. The hook can't assume either state.

### His Inner World

**What he secretly wants to hear:** "You're doing a good job — but check what you're measuring yourself against." (Stop comparing to the 5-minute version you see of other dads.) And: "You can get there. You can reduce the stress and anxiety. It won't all be wins. But look inward first."

**Why he hasn't joined a community before:**
- Doesn't believe reading papers will change him
- "Who is this guy? What does he know?"
- Not ready to change habits
- Seven-day challenge energy — too gimmicky, too shallow
- Nothing deep enough to keep him in (Oren's own experience as a member of other communities)

**Instant dealbreaker:**
- Heavy intellectual framing up front — Aurelius, universe, philosophy named before felt — sounds like a lecture, feels detached
- Too pushy — especially lethal for someone who's a bit withdrawn and not high on confidence
- Wall of text
- Design/font that feels off
- ⚡ KEY INSIGHT: **The door must be visual, not textual.** For this audience, an animation or cartoon that shows a scene he recognizes pulls him in without asking anything of him. No reading required, no listening required — just recognition. That's the format to test first.

## OREN'S VOICE — OBSERVED IN THIS INTERVIEW
*(Captured live from how he actually speaks — not asked directly. More detailed Section 10 answers pending.)*

### How he talks
- **Speaks in layers.** He starts with a statement, then circles back and adds depth, then connects it to something bigger. Never linear.
- **Grounds everything in personal observation.** Not theory — what he saw, felt, experienced. Heavy "I" usage. Personal, not prescriptive.
- **Thinks at universe scale, then lands in a specific moment.** He'll say "nature has no redundancy" and then tell you about a snack bar. That zoom-in/zoom-out is his natural cadence.
- **Honest about gaps.** "I don't have all the answers." "I'm kind of in the way." He doesn't perform certainty.
- **Optimistic underneath the struggle.** Even when describing hard things, there's a current of "we can get there" running underneath.
- **Anti-prescriptive.** Never gives you "5 steps." Gives you a frame, a feeling, a question.

### His phrases and framings
- "The universe" — used constantly as the reference point for what's real and true
- "Calibrate" — things calibrate themselves, kids calibrate through feedback, you calibrate your measuring stick
- "Spark" — the filter for who belongs in Dads'42
- "Embrace the challenge, cherish the moments" — his parenting philosophy
- "Don't panic" — borrowed from Adams, now owned
- "Pillars" — his framework for what he wants his kids to be
- "Look inward" — his prescription for almost everything
- "The universe rewards" / "reality mirrors back" — he trusts the feedback loop of the world

### His duality (the brand personality)
One leg in the eternal and serious (Aurelius, Machin, the universe, evolution).
One leg in the naughty and playful (Adams, Hunt, humor, irreverence).
That tension is not a contradiction — it's the whole point. A dad who takes fatherhood seriously AND doesn't take himself too seriously.

### What to avoid when writing in his voice
- Don't be prescriptive or listy ("5 ways to...")
- Don't be heavy-academic upfront (name the idea before the feeling)
- Don't be pushy or urgent in tone
- Don't perform certainty he doesn't have
- Don't be preachy — he asks questions more than he gives answers
- Don't use "cheesy" LinkedIn language (he tried it, it felt wrong immediately)

### The voice in one sentence
*Grounded in science, lit by philosophy, carrying the humor of a man who knows the universe is 42 — and still showing up for his kids every day.*
